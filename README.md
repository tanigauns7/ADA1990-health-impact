# The Causal Effect of the Americans with Disabilities Act (1990) on Healthcare Access
Difference-in-differences analysis of the Americans with Disabilities Act (1990) on healthcare access for people with disabilities using NHIS data.                    
  
This paper estimates the causal effect of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) of 1990 on healthcare access for individuals with disabilities in the United States. Using a difference-in-differences design that exploits the Act's enactment as a quasi-natural experiment, I compare changes in healthcare utilization and access outcomes for disabled individuals relative to non-disabled individuals before and after 1990.

 The ADA prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in employment, public accommodations, and services — including healthcare facilities. While its civil rights provisions are well-documented, its downstream effects on actual healthcare access remain underexplored. This analysis leverages survey microdata to identify whether the ADA's mandates translated into measurable
  improvements in coverage, utilization, and reported access barriers.
  
Key methods: Difference-in-differences (DiD), parallel trends testing, robustness checks with alternative control groups.
